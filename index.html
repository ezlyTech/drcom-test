The importance of controlling BP variability in hypertension treatment

BP variability can be an accompanying or independent risk factor targeted in antihypertension therapy.1 So, what is BP variability and how it might be a concern for your hypertensive patients?

What is BP variability

BP variability is a complex phenomenon, classifiable into different types1-3

  • Very short-term variability (beat-to-beat)
  • Short-term variability (in 24 h):

In normal subjects (dippers), nocturnal reduction of systolic and diastolic BP is about 10-20%. In hypertensive subjects (non-dippers), this reduction is <10%, while in the “reverse dippers”, BP can increase during the nights.
An increased BP in the first hours of the morning is called “morning rise/surge”.

How to measure BP variability

The precise measurement of BP variability is also complex and controversial1,3

  • Very short-term variability: Penaz method, based on finger sensors
  • Short-term variability: non-invasive 24-h ambulatory measurement.
    • Indices such as the real average variability, residual variability or weighted 24h BP deviation have been used to eliminate the influence of stressors and day-night differences.
  • Medium-term variability: home BP monitoring.
    • A variation on BP levels could arise between assessments in the office or in the out-of-office settings (white-coat hypertension).4 To address this, home BP self-monitoring is recommended as a practical method for hypertension diagnosis and treatment monitoring.5

What is the impact of BP variability?1

Prognostic and clinical significance of BP variability

24h BP variability reflects sympathetic activation and impairment of baroceptive reflexes. BP variability was associated with increased prognosis of: 1,2,6,7

  • Cardiac, vascular, and renal subclinical organ damage2,6
  • CV events and mortality2,6,7
  • All cause mortality7

Application in relevant clinical practice

In clinical practice, the effect of antihypertensive drugs on BP variability can be assessed by: 1

  • trough/peak ratio
  • smoothness index

Strategies of controlling BP variability include:

  • Drug selection: Drugs with a long duration of action and homogeneous effect over 24h are most likely to reduce BP variations1
  • Timing of administration, i.e., bedtime administration for non-dippers (subjects without nocturnal BP fall)3,8
  • Treatment adherence: Ensure patients do not omit doses, delay or take improper dosing; follow titration schedules and adhere strictly to treatment1,3

BP variability can provide useful complementary information in terms of possible prognosis and treatment effectiveness. However, we still need a better understanding of its relationship with CV risk and therapeutic implications.
